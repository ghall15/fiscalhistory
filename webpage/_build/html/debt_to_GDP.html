The Debt to GDP Ratio¶

Suppose your credit card balance is $20,000. Is this a big balance?

Well it depends

  • If your annual income is $15,000, then yes.
  • If your annual income is $1,500,000, then no.

It also depends on how fast your income is growing.

So we might be interested in the ratio of debt-to-income. Let’s denote income by \(Y\), and the ratio of debt-to-income by \(\frac{B}{Y}\).

We plotted the ratio of U.S. government debt to GDP in first lecture.

The Evolution of the Debt to GDP Ratio¶

Ignore seignorage for now and set transfer to zero. So we can write the government budget constraint as

\[\begin{split}B_{t} &=& B_{t-1} + r B_{t-1} + G_t - T_t \\ &=& (1+r) B_{t-1} + G_t - T_t\end{split}\]

Divide both sides of the equation by current income \(Y_t\)

\[\begin{split}\frac{B_{t}}{Y_t} &=& (1+r) \frac{B_{t-1}}{Y_t} + \frac{G_t - T_t}{Y_t} \\ &=& (1+r) \frac{B_{t-1}}{Y_t}\frac{Y_{t-1}}{Y_{t-1}} + \frac{G_t - T_t}{Y_t} \\ &=& (1+r) \frac{B_{t-1}}{Y_{t-1}}\frac{Y_{t-1}}{Y_{t}} + \frac{G_t - T_t}{Y_t}\end{split}\]

Let \(g\) denote the percentage change in \(Y\)

\[g = \frac{Y_t}{Y_{t-1}} - 1\]

so

\[\frac{Y_{t-1}}{Y_t} = \frac{1}{1+g}\]

So we can write

\[\begin{split}\frac{B_{t}}{Y_t} &=& \frac{(1+r)}{(1+g)} \frac{B_{t-1}}{Y_{t-1}} + \frac{G_t - T_t}{Y_t}.\end{split}\]

Then by a handy math fact [1] we attain the law of motion of the debt-to-GDP ratio.

\[\begin{split}\frac{B_{t}}{Y_t} &\approx& (1+r-g) \frac{B_{t-1}}{Y_{t-1}} + \frac{G_t - T_t}{Y_t}\end{split}\]

This equation characterizes the evolution of the debt-to-GDP ratio. The evolution depends on the interest rate, \(r\), the growth rate of GDP, \(g\), and the primary deficit \(G-T\). The higher the interest rate, the more resources the government must devote toward compensating its creditors, hence the larger will be next period’s value of \(\frac{B}{Y}\). In contract a larger value of \(g\) implies that the denominator is growing faster diminishing next period’s value of The higher the primary deficit the larger the necessary increase in the debt and the larger the value of next period’s \(\frac{B}{Y}\).

Sustainability¶

Most countries have positive debt-to-GDP ratios. In the short-term, it is often no big deal if this ratio rises. But can debt-to-GDP ratios rise forever?

In other words, which paths of \(B/Y\) are stable? which are explosive?

To keep the math simple, assume \(G\) and \(T\) grow at rate \(g\) so that \(\frac{G_t - T_t}{Y_t}\) is a constant.

\[\frac{B_{t}}{Y_t} = (1+r-g) \frac{B_{t-1}}{Y_{t-1}} + \frac{G - T}{Y}\]

It is relatively simple to study this relationship in EXCEL.

An Example in EXCEL¶

Consider the following case. Set

  • \(g\), the growth rate of \(Y\), to 3 percent
  • \(r\), the interest rate, to 2 percent
  • \(\frac{G - T}{Y}\), the ratio of the primary deficit to GDP, to 5 percent, and
  • the initial value of the debt to GDP ratio \(\frac{B_0}{Y_0}\) to 10 percent.

We can then simulate the path of the debt-to-GDP.

We see that the debt-to-GDP ratio is growing each period. Does this mean it will grow forever?

Let’s extend the simulation for 500 periods. Then we plot the 500 simulations of \(\frac{B}{Y}\) computed in column B.

We see that the debt-to-GDP ratio grows, but that this growth diminishes as the series approaches 5.

Now let’s change this exercise by raising the interest rate from 2 percent to 4 percent holding everything else fixed.

In this case, the debt-to-GDP explodes toward infinity.

So what is going on? Let’s go back to the algebra.

Back to the Algebra¶

Consider two cases

  1. \(g>r\) (i.e. the growth rate of GDP exceeds the interest rate)
  2. \(g \le r\) (i.e. the growth rate of GDP is less than the interest rate)

Let’s plot the law of motion for the debt-to-GDP ratio for case 1.

Determining the Steady-State Debt-to-GDP Ratio when g > r

In this figure, the thick black line represents the right hand side of the law of motion and the thin red line corresponds to the 45 degree line. At every point on the 45 degree line, \(\frac{B_{t-1}}{Y_{t-1}} =\frac{B_t}{Y_t}\).

These two lines intersect once with the black line crossing the 45 degree line only once from above. This single crossing occurs because

  1. the y-intercept of the law of motion, the primary deficit to GDP ratio, is positive, and
  2. the slope of the law of motion \((1-g+r)\) is less than one (i.e. the slope of the 45 degree line)

The intersection of these two lines determines the steady-state value of \(\frac{B}{Y}\). At the steady state, \(\frac{B^*}{Y^*}\), the debt-to-GDP ratio remains constant.

The steady state corresponds to:

\[\begin{split}\frac{B^*}{Y^*} &=& (1+r-g) \frac{B^*}{Y^*} + \frac{G - T}{Y} \\ (g-r) \frac{B^*}{Y^*} &=& \frac{G - T}{Y} \\ \frac{B^*}{Y^*} &=& \frac{1}{g-r} \frac{G - T}{Y}\end{split}\]

Transitioning to the Steady-State Debt-to-GDP Ratio when g > r

Consider an economy that starts with a debt-to-GDP ratio of \(B_0/Y_0.\) In the next period, the debt-to-GDP ratio becomes

\[\frac{B_{1}}{Y_1} = (1+r-g) \frac{B_{0}}{Y_{0}} + \frac{G - T}{Y}\]

This one step is represented in the figure above by the vertical solid blue line from point \((B_0/Y_0,0)\) on the axis to the point \((B_0/Y_0,B_1/Y_1)\) on the black line.

We can represent the evolution of the debt-to-GDP ratio from \(B_1/Y_1\) to \(B_2/Y_2\) in a similar fashion. To find the point \((B_1/Y_1,0)\) on the x-axis, we move from the point \((B_0/Y_0,B_1/Y_1)\) on the black line to point \((B_1/Y_1,B_1/Y_1)\) on the red 45 degree line.

Once again we apply the equation

\[\frac{B_{2}}{Y_2} = (1+r-g) \frac{B_{1}}{Y_{1}} + \frac{G - T}{Y}\]

to find point \((B_1/Y_1,B_2/Y_2)\) on the black line.

This process repeats itself for an infinite number of periods as the debt-to-GDP approach the steady-state debt-to-GDP ratio \(\frac{B^*}{Y^*}\) from below.

In a similar fashion, if the country starts with a debt-to-GDP ratio greater than \(\frac{B^*}{Y^*}\) (for example at \(\frac{b_0}{y_0}\) as shown in the figure above) the debt-to-GDP ratio will converge to the steady-state from above.

Now let’s plot the law of motion for debt-to-GDP ratio for case 2.

No Steady-State Debt-to-GDP Ratio Exists when g < r

As in the prior figure, the thick black curve represents the right hand side of the law of motion and the thin red line corresponded to the 45 degree line.

In this case, the slope of the law of motion is greater than one, so the two curves do not intersect. Thus no steady state exists.

Again, consider an economy that starts with a debt-to-GDP ratio of \(B_0/Y_0.\) In the next period, the debt-to-GDP ratio becomes

\[\frac{B_{1}}{Y_1} = (1+r-g) \frac{B_{0}}{Y_{0}} + \frac{G - T}{Y}\]

This one step is represented in the figure above by the vertical solid blue line from point \((B_0/Y_0,0)\) on the axis to the point \((B_0/Y_0,B_1/Y_1)\) on the black line.

As in the prior example, we represent the evolution of the debt-to-GDP ratio from \(B_1/Y_1\) to \(B_2/Y_2\) by first finding the point \((B_1/Y_1,0)\) on the x-axis by moving from the point \((B_0/Y_0,B_1/Y_1)\) on the black line to point \((B_1/Y_1,B_1/Y_1)\) on the red 45 degree line and then applying the equation

\[\frac{B_{2}}{Y_2} = (1+r-g) \frac{B_{1}}{Y_{1}} + \frac{G - T}{Y}\]

to find point \((B_1/Y_1,B_2/Y_2)\) on the black line.

But unlike the previous case, when \(g < r\) the increase in debt-to-GDP ratio from \(\frac{B_{1}}{Y_{1}}\) to \(\frac{B_{2}}{Y_{2}}\) is greater than the increase from \(\frac{B_{0}}{Y_{0}}\) to \(\frac{B_{1}}{Y_{1}}\).

Instead of converging to a steady state, the debt-to-GDP ratio will sail off to infinity.

Take-Away Points¶

The two take-aways from this exercise are

  1. If a country grows faster than its interest rate (i.e if g > r), its debt-to-GDP ratio will not blow up.
  2. Small changes in the interest rate and/or growth rate of GDP can have a dramatic impact on the path of the debt-to-GDP ratio.

A government can sustain persistent deficits as long as growth in output is greater than the real interest rate. A country does not necessarily need a balanced budget in order to insure its debt-to-GDP rate is sustainable. This is not to say that the debt-to-GDP will not be quite high. In our Excel example, the steady-state debt-to-GDP was 500 percent. But as long as \(g > r\), the debt-to-GDP will remain finite.

Debt crises occur when a country switches from having \(g > r\) to having \(g < r\).

Since World War II, the U.S. has generally grown faster than the interest rate the government has faced. On average the U.S. has grown about 3.3 percent per year. The average interest rate on government debt has been about 1.6 percent.

Exercises¶

  1. A government initially has a debt-to-gdp ratio of 0.3. Starting now, the government runs a primary budget deficit of 3 percent of GDP every year forever. It borrows at a real interest rate of 3 percent. Real GDP of the economy grows at an annual rate of 5 percent.

    1. In the long run, to what value will the debt-to-gdp ratio converge?
    2. In the long run, what will be the growth rate of \(B\)?
    3. Will the size of the year-to-year changes in the debt-to-gdp ratio increase or decrease over time? Explain briefly.

2. Suppose a country has a debt-to-GDP ratio of 100%. The growth rate of real GDP is 3%. Assume that the real interest rate is 2%. If the government is committed to not letting the debt-to-GDP ratio rise, what is the largest primary deficit as a percent of GDP it can run?

\[\log (1 + x) \approx x \; \mbox{ for ``small''} \; x's\]
  • Why? Taylor Series (Newton)
\[\begin{split}f(x) &=& \log(1+x)\\ &\approx& f(x_0) + (x-x_0)f'(x_0) |_{x=x_0}\end{split}\]
  • Apply with \(f(x) = \log(1+x)\) and \(x_0 = 0\), then
\[\begin{split}\log(1+x) &\approx& 0 + x\left(\frac{1}{1+x}\right) |_{x=x_0} \\ &\approx& x\end{split}\]
