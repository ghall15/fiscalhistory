The Government Budget Constraint¶

Your Credit Card Bill¶

If you have a credit card, you get a bill each month.

Your balance this month is

(1)\[\begin{split}\mbox{Balance this month} &=& \mbox{Balance last month} \\ && \; + \; \mbox{ Finance Charge} \\ && \; + \; \mbox{ New Purchases} \\ && \; - \; \mbox{ Payments}\end{split}\]

Let’s re-write this as

\[B_t = B_{t-1} + r B_{t-1} + G_t - T_t\]

where we let

  • \(B\) denote the balance,
  • \(r\) denote the monthly interest rate (so \(rB\) is the finance charge),
  • \(G\) be new purchases, and
  • \(T\) be payments.

Stock Variables and Flow Variables¶

Stock variables are measured at a point in time.

Flow variables are measured over a period of time.

So for your credit card

  • \(B\) is a stock variable, and
  • \(r B\), \(G\), and \(T\) are flow variables.

The Government Budget Constraint¶

A sovereign government faces a budget constraint that must hold at each period, \(t\):

\[B_{t} = B_{t-1} + r B_{t-1} + G_t + TR_t - T_t\]

where

\[\begin{split}G_t &=& \mbox{government spending during period } t \\ TR_t &=& \mbox{transfer payments (e.g. social security) during period } t \\ T_t &=& \mbox{taxes collected during period } t \\ B_{t} &=& \mbox{this period's } t \mbox{ government debt} \\ r &=& \mbox{real interest rate}\end{split}\]

Many macro textbooks write it this way

\[B_{t} = B_{t-1} + INT_t + G_t + TR_t - T_t\]

  • Prior to 1790

  • 1790-present

    • Office of Management and Budget (1934-present)

  • 1929-present

    • National Income and Product Accounts (NIPA)

NIPA vs Treasury/OMB¶

  • NIPA

    • calendar year/quarter, 1929-present

    • consistent accounting with state/local as well as GDP

    • main spending categories

      • Government consumption, \(G_t\)
      • Transfer payments, \(TR_t\)
      • Net interest payments, \(INT_t\)

  • Treasury/OMB

    • fiscal year

    • spending divided by function

      • pre-1940: war, navy, pensions, indians, interest, ...
      • 1934-present: national defense, human resources, physical resources, net interest, ...

Deficits and Surpluses¶

The budget deficit that gets reported in the newspaper is

\[G_t + TR_t + INT_t - T_t\]

When this number is positive there is a budget deficit.

When this number is negative there is a budget surplus.

Change in the debt is equal to the deficit

\[B_{t} - B_{t-1} = INT_t + G_t + TR_t - T_t\]

The net-of-interest deficit or primary deficit is

\[G_t + TR_t - T_t\]

The total or gross deficit tells the amount the government must borrow to cover all of its expenditures.

The primary deficit ignores interest payments

The Printing Press¶

Since the signing of the U.S. Constitution, the Federal government can raise revenue by issuing money.

State and local governments can not.

For the Federal government

\[B_{t} = B_{t-1} + r B_{t-1} + G_t + TR_t - T_t - \frac{M_t - M_{t-1}}{P_t}\]

where

\[\begin{split}M_{t}-M_{t-1} &=& \mbox{New money printed this period.} \\ P_t &=& \mbox{Price level this period ($t$)} \\ & & \mbox{i.e. the relative price of money in terms of goods}\end{split}\]

This additional term is called {em seignorage} or the inflation tax.

So the government budget constraint becomes

\[\begin{split}B_{t} &=& B_{t-1} + r B_{t-1} + G_t + TR_t - T_t - \frac{M_t - M_{t-1}}{P_t}\end{split}\]

Write it as

\[B_{t} - B_{t-1} + \frac{M_t - M_{t-1}}{P_t} = r B_{t-1} + G_t + TR_t - T_t\]

Decisions about \(G_t\), \(TR_t\) and \(T_t\) are called fiscal policy

Decision about \(B_t\) and :math`M_t` are called monetary policy.

Debt-to-GDP Ratio¶

Suppose the credit card balance is \(\$20,000\). Is this a big balance?

  • Well it depends
    • If your annual income is \(\$15,000\), then yes.
    • If your annual income is \(\$1,500,000\), then no.
  • It also depends on how fast your income is growing.

So we might be interested in the ratio of debt-to-income. Let’s call income \(Y\), and the ratio of debt-to-income \(\frac{B}{Y}\).

A Digression on the U.S. Debt¶

See the U.S. Treasury Monthly Statement of the Public Debt.

  • Marketable Debt
    • Treasury bills, notes, and bonds
    • can be bought and sold on secondary markets
  • Non-marketable Debt
    • no secondary market – saving bonds, state and local governments
  • Held by the Public
    • you, me, China
  • Held inside the government
    • Social Security Trust Fund

Ignore seignorage for now, set \(TR = 0\).

\[\begin{split}B_{t} &=& B_{t-1} + r B_{t-1} + G_t - T_t \\ &=& (1+r) B_{t-1} + G_t - T_t\end{split}\]

Divide both sides of the equation by current income \(Y_t\)

\[\begin{split}\frac{B_{t}}{Y_t} &=& (1+r) \frac{B_{t-1}}{Y_t} + \frac{G_t - T_t}{Y_t} \\ &=& (1+r) \frac{B_{t-1}}{Y_t}\frac{Y_{t-1}}{Y_{t-1}} + \frac{G_t - T_t}{Y_t} \\ &=& (1+r) \frac{B_{t-1}}{Y_{t-1}}\frac{Y_{t-1}}{Y_{t}} + \frac{G_t - T_t}{Y_t}\end{split}\]

Let \(g\) denote the percentage change in \(Y\)

\[g = \frac{Y_t}{Y_{t-1}} - 1\]

so

\[\frac{Y_{t-1}}{Y_t} = \frac{1}{1+g}\]

So we can write

\[\begin{split}\frac{B_{t}}{Y_t} &=& \frac{(1+r)}{(1+g)} \frac{B_{t-1}}{Y_{t-1}} + \frac{G_t - T_t}{Y_t} \\ &\approx& (1+r-g) \frac{B_{t-1}}{Y_{t-1}} + \frac{G_t - T_t}{Y_t} \\\end{split}\]

To keep the math simple, assume \(G\) and \(T\) grow at rate \(g\).

Most countries have positive debt-to-GDP ratios. In the short-term, it is often no big deal if this ratio rises.

But can debt-to-GDP ratios rise forever?

  • debt crisis
  • currency crisis
  • hyperinflation

In other words, which paths of \(B/Y\) are stable? which are explosive?

\[\frac{B_{t}}{Y_t} = (1+r-g) \frac{B_{t-1}}{Y_{t-1}} + \frac{G - T}{Y_t}\]

Consider two cases

  1. \(g>r\)
  2. \(g \le r\)

If :math`g > r`, the debt-to-GDP ratio will not blow up. So a government can sustain persistent deficits as long as growth in output is greater than the real interest rate.

In the U.S. \(r = 0.016\) and \(g = 0.033\).

Don’t necessarily need a balanced budget.
