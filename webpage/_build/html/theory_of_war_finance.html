begin{frame}{How Should a Country Finance a War?}

The government’s budget constraint begin{eqnarray*} {B_t} = sum_{s=0}^{infty} left(frac{1}{1+r}right)^s ( T_{t+s} - G_{t+s} ). end{eqnarray*}

medskip

If an unexpected increase in spending occurs, the government can do three things: begin{enumerate} item cut spending somewhere else, item raise taxes now or later item impose a capital loss on the bond holders end{enumerate}

end{frame}

begin{frame}{Debt or taxes?}

begin{itemize}

item Consider a one-period example

item Assume no foreign borrowing or lending

item The private sector (i.e. households) budget constraint: begin{eqnarray*} Y-T = C + S_{pvt} end{eqnarray*} where begin{eqnarray*} Y &=& mbox{income}\ T &=& mbox{lump-sum taxes}\ C &=& mbox{consumption}\ S_{pvt} &=& mbox{private saving} end{eqnarray*}

end{itemize}

end{frame}

begin{frame}

begin{itemize}

item Government saving, $S_{gvt}$, is just the opposite of the deficit begin{eqnarray*} S_{gvt} = T - G end{eqnarray*}

item National saving begin{eqnarray*} S &=& S_{pvt} + S_{gvt} \

&=& Y-T - C + T - G \ &=& Y - C - G

end{eqnarray*}

item Since national saving must be zero: $ Y = C + G$

end{itemize}

end{frame}

begin{frame}{Consider a Two-Period Example}

begin{itemize}

item Assumptions begin{enumerate} item lump-sum taxes item no money, so no inflation item no uncertainty end{enumerate}

medskip

item Consider an environment in which households live for two periods: begin{itemize} item period 1: now item period 2: the future end{itemize}

end{itemize}

end{frame}

begin{frame}

begin{itemize}

item Assume an individual gets happiness from consumption ($C_t$) in each period. So happiness is a function of $C_1$ and $C_2$.

item In period 1, the individual has some after-tax income $Y_1 - T_1$ so her budget constraint is: begin{eqnarray*} C_1 + S_{pvt} = Y_1 - T_1 end{eqnarray*} item In period 2, the individual has after-tax income $Y_2 - T_2$ plus her savings from the previous period begin{eqnarray*} C_2 = Y_2-T_2 + (1+r)S_{pvt} end{eqnarray*} Note $S_{pvt}$ can be positive or negative.

item We can substitute out for $S_{pvt}$. begin{eqnarray*} C_2 = Y_2 - T_2 + (1+r)(Y_1 - T_1 - C_1) end{eqnarray*}

end{itemize} end{frame}

begin{frame} begin{itemize}

item Rearranging we get $$C_1 + frac{C_2}{1+r} = Y_1 - T_1 + frac{Y_2 - T_2}{1+r}$$

medskip

item Everything in this equation is units of ``goods in period 1’‘. The term $frac{1}{1+r}$ is relative price of goods between the two periods and converts the units from the second period into the first.

medskip

item This budget line equates the present value of lifetime consumption (PVLC) to the present value of lifetime resources (PVLR). end{itemize}

end{frame}

begin{frame}{The household’s budget constraint}

Let’s think about the problem graphically. begin{itemize} item The budget constraint can be written in $y = b + mx$ form. begin{eqnarray*} C_2 = (1+r) (Y_1 - T_1) + (Y_2 -T_2) - (1+r)C_1 end{eqnarray*}

item What is the opportunity cost of additional consumption in period 1? of additional consumption in period
2?

end{itemize}

end{frame}

begin{frame}{Household Preferences for Consumption Over Time}

begin{itemize}

item Households prefer a little bit of consumption each period rather than a lot one period and a little the
other.

item Consider your eating patterns ...

item For now, let’s take this assumption to the extreme.

item Assume households always choose begin{eqnarray*} C_1 = C_2 end{eqnarray*}

item Measured private saving is $$ S_{pvt} = (Y_1 - T_1) - C_1 $$

end{itemize}

end{frame}

begin{frame}{Now Add a Government}

begin{itemize}

item The government spends G in period 1 and nothing in period 2.

medskip

item The government can pay for it across two periods. So the present value government budget constraint is

$$ G = T_1 + frac{T_2}{1+r}.$$

medskip

item The government has to raise the revenue to cover this expenditures. Consider two policy options: begin{enumerate} item tax finance: set $T_1 = G$, $T_2 = 0$ item debt finance: set $T_1 = 0$, $T_2 = (1+r)G$ end{enumerate}

end{itemize}

end{frame}

begin{frame}

Will these two financing arrangements have different effects on the household’s consumption patterns? Let’s think through the intuition begin{itemize} item Taxes just reduce the household’s after-tax income $Y_1 - T_1$ and/or $Y_2 - T_2$.

smallskip

item Both policies are going to reduce the PVLR by the same amount.

smallskip

item So the household doesn’t care which one of these policies the government implements. end{itemize}

end{frame}

begin{frame}{Ricardian Equivalence}

begin{itemize}

item Robert Barro

medskip

item Ricardian equivalence implies that the timing of taxes is irrelevant on household consumption choices.

medskip

item A change in current taxes, exactly offset in present-value terms by an equal and opposite change in future
taxes will have no effect on the optimal choices of $C_1$ and $C_2$.

medskip

item A tax cut this period, leaving government spending unchanged, will decrease government saving this
period.

But this decrease in government saving will be exactly offset by an increase in private saving, leaving national saving unchanged.

end{itemize}

end{frame}

begin{frame}{Robert Barro’s Model of Tax Smoothing}

begin{itemize}

item Robert J. Barro ``On the Determination of the Public Debt”, {em Journal of Political Economy}, Vol. 87, No. 5, Part 1 (Oct., 1979), pp. 940-971

medskip

item Infinite horizon

medskip

item Tax create distortions (i.e. inefficiencies) which increase at an increasing rate with the tax rate.

medskip

item Government would like to minimize these inefficiencies.

end{itemize}

end{frame}

begin{frame}{Tax Smoothing}

begin{itemize}

item First, consider a deterministic setting. begin{itemize} item That is, the path of government purchases ($G_t$) is given end{itemize}

medskip

item Assume the real interest rate ($r$) is fixed.

medskip

item The initial outstanding stock of debt $B_0$ is some number (perhaps zero).

end{itemize}

end{frame}

begin{frame}

begin{itemize}

item The government budget constraint is: begin{eqnarray*} B_t = (1+r) B_{t-1} + G_t - T_t end{eqnarray*}

item In present value form begin{eqnarray*} sum_{t=1}^{infty} frac{G_t}{(1+r)^t} + B_0 = sum_{t=1}^{infty} frac{T_t}{(1+r)^t} end{eqnarray*}

item The government want to choose the path of taxes $T_t$ and debt $B_t$ to satisfy its budget constraint while minimizing the present value of the costs of distortions that the taxes create.

end{itemize}

end{frame}

begin{frame}

begin{itemize} item Consider government choose pathes of taxes and debt to minimize $$ sum_{t=0}^{infty} left(frac{1}{1+r}right)^t left( phi_1 T_t + frac{1}{2} phi_2 T_t^2 right).$$ item The government budget constraint is $$ B_t = (1+r) B_{t-1} + G_t - T_t.$$

item Assume $G_t$ is a deterministic process.

item Assume $B_0$ = 0.

end{itemize}

end{frame}

begin{frame}

begin{itemize} item Set up the Lagrangian {footnotesize begin{eqnarray*} {cal L} = min_{T_t, B_t} sum_{t=0}^{infty} left(frac{1}{1+r}right)^t left( phi_1 T_t + frac{1}{2} phi_2 T_t^2 + lambda_t( B_t - (1+r) B_{t-1} - G_t + T_t)right) end{eqnarray*}}

item Take first order conditions begin{eqnarray*} frac{partial cal L}{partial T_t} &=& phi_1 + phi_2 T_t - lambda_t = 0 ; ; forall t \ frac{partial cal L}{partial B_t} &=& lambda_t - frac{1}{1+r} (1+r) lambda_{t+1} = 0 ; ; forall t \ frac{partial cal L}{partial lambda_t} &=& B_t - (1+r) B_{t-1} - G_t + T_t = 0 ; ; forall t \ end{eqnarray*}

end{itemize}

end{frame}

begin{frame}

begin{itemize}

item The first two FOCs imply begin{eqnarray*} T_t = T_{t+1} ; ; forall t end{eqnarray*}

item Taxes must be constant. A smooth tax rate minimizes distortions over time.

end{itemize}

end{frame}

begin{frame}{Tax Smoothing in a stochastic setting}

begin{itemize}

item Now consider an environment in which $G_t$ follows a stochastic process.

item The government’s problem becomes begin{eqnarray*} min_{T_t} E sum_{t=1}^{infty} left(frac{1}{1+r}right)^t left( phi_1 T_t + frac{1}{2} phi_2 T_t^2 right) end{eqnarray*} subject to begin{eqnarray*} sum_{t=1}^{infty} frac{G_t}{(1+r)^t} + B_0 = sum_{t=1}^{infty} frac{T_t}{(1+r)^t} end{eqnarray*}

item The $E$ stands for expectation.

end{itemize}

end{frame}

begin{frame}

{footnotesize Again we can set up a Lagrangian begin{eqnarray*} {cal L} = min_{T_t, B_t} E_0 sum_{t=0}^{infty} left(frac{1}{1+r}right)^t left( phi_1 T_t + frac{1}{2} phi_2 T_t^2 + lambda_t( B_t - (1+r) B_{t-1} - G_t + T_t)right) end{eqnarray*}

Take first order conditions begin{eqnarray*} frac{partial cal L}{partial T_t} &=& phi_1 + phi_2 T_t - lambda_t = 0 ; ; forall t \ frac{partial cal L}{partial B_t} &=& lambda_t - E_t frac{1}{1+r} (1+r) lambda_{t+1} = 0 ; ; forall t \ frac{partial cal L}{partial lambda_t} &=& B_t - (1+r) B_{t-1} - G_t + T_t = 0 ; ; forall t \ end{eqnarray*}

medskip

The first two FOCs imply begin{eqnarray*} T_t = E_t T_{t+1} ; ; forall t end{eqnarray*} {em Tax rates follow a random walk. There cannot be predictable changes in the tax rate.}}

end{frame}

begin{frame}{Implications}

begin{itemize}

item This implies $E_t T_{t+s} = T_t$ for any $s > 0$.

medskip

item Consider again the government budget constraint. begin{eqnarray*} sum_{t=1}^{infty} frac{G_t}{(1+r)^t} + B_0 = sum_{t=1}^{infty} frac{T_t}{(1+r)^t} end{eqnarray*} or begin{eqnarray*} B_0 = E_t sum_{t=1}^{infty} frac{T_t - G_t}{(1+r)^t} end{eqnarray*}

medskip

item So we can write the GBC as begin{eqnarray*} B_t = frac{T}{r} - E_t sum_{s=0}^{infty} frac{G_{t+s}}{(1+r)^{s+1}} end{eqnarray*}

end{itemize}

end{frame}

begin{frame}{A Temporary Increase in Government Expenditures}

Suppose government spending follows the stochastic process begin{eqnarray*} G_t &=& bar{G} + epsilon_t \ E_t epsilon_{t+1} &=& 0. end{eqnarray*} So shocks are purely transitory.

end{frame}

begin{frame}{A Temporary Increase in Government Expenditures (con’t)}

Then begin{eqnarray*} B_t &=& frac{T_t}{r} - E_t sum_{s=0}^{infty} frac{G_{t+s}}{(1+r)^{s+1}} \

&=& frac{T_t}{r} - frac{bar{G}}{r} - frac{epsilon_t}{1+r}

end{eqnarray*} so begin{eqnarray*} T_t = bar{G} + rB_t + frac{r}{1+r} epsilon_t end{eqnarray*} and begin{eqnarray*} B_{t+1} = frac{T_{t+1}}{r} - frac{bar{G}}{r} - frac{epsilon_{t+1}}{1+r} end{eqnarray*}

end{frame}

begin{frame}{A Temporary Increase in Government Expenditures (con’t)}

so begin{eqnarray*} E_t B_{t+1} &=& frac{E_t T_{t+1}}{r} - frac{bar{G}}{r} \

&=& frac{T_t}{r} - frac{bar{G}}{r} \ &=& B_t + frac{epsilon_{t+1}}{1+r}

end{eqnarray*}

medskip

This holds for all subsequent periods begin{eqnarray*} E_t B_{t+n} &=& B_t + frac{epsilon_{t+1}}{1+r} end{eqnarray*}

end{frame}

begin{frame}{A Permanent Increase in Government Expenditures}

begin{itemize}

item Suppose government spending increases by $Delta G$ permanently.

item The government budget constraint is begin{eqnarray*} (1+r) B_{t+s} = T_{t+s} - (bar{G} + Delta G) + B_{t+s+1} ; ; forall s ge 0 end{eqnarray*}

item If we want to smooth taxes $T_{t+s} = T_t$, then: begin{eqnarray*} (1+r) B_{t+s} = T_{t} - (bar{G} + Delta G) + B_{t+s+1} ; ; forall s ge 0 end{eqnarray*} So $B_{t+s} = B_{t+s+1} $ for all $s$, so debt must stay constant.

item Entire shock is absorbed by the tax rate. Taxes rise by $Delta G$.

end{itemize} end{frame}

begin{frame}{How much did the government really pay to borrow money?} scriptsize In a 1830 U.S. House Committee on Ways and Means Report, Mr. M’Duffie reckoned that during the War of 1812, the Government paid $46 million to borrow $80 million. On page 12 of the report he states: begin{quote} The government borrowed, during the short period of the war, eighty millions of dollars, at an average discount of fifteen per cent. giving certificates of stock, amounting to eighty millions of dollars, in exchange for sixty-eight millions of dollars, in such bank paper as could be obtained. In this statement, treasury notes are considered as stock, at twenty per cent. discount. Upon the very face of the transaction, therefore, there was a loss of twelve millions of dollars, ... But the sum of sixty-eight millions of dollars, received by the government, was in a depreciated currency, not more than half as valuable as that in which the stock given in exchange for it, has been and will be redeemed. Here, then, is another loss of thirty-four millions, resulting, incontestibly and exclusively, from the depreciation of the currency, and making, with the sum lost by the discount, forty-six millions of dollars. end{quote} end{frame}

begin{frame}{War Patterns}

In almost every war, you see the following

begin{enumerate}

item Early on, the war promises to be short and inexpensive.

item Early on, rely on short-term financing

item Wars are rarely short and cheap, need for more revenue requires tax increases and long-term lending

item After the war expenditures rarely fall to pre-war levels begin{itemize} item interest payments item pension item other stuff end{itemize}

end{enumerate}
